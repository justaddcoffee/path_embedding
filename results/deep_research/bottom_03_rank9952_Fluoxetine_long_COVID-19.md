**Dexamethasone (Drug):** Dexamethasone is a potent **glucocorticoid steroid** with powerful anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive properties. **Long COVID-19 (Disease):** “Long COVID,” formally known as **post-acute sequelae of COVID-19 (PASC)**, refers to a syndrome of persistent or new health issues that continue for weeks or months after the initial SARS-CoV-2 infection has cleared. ## Mechanistic Links in the Proposed Pathway

### 1. Fluoxetine’s Effects on EGFR Signaling  
There is **no direct classical pathway** in which Fluoxetine binds to or directly regulates the EGFR gene. However, Fluoxetine does influence intracellular signaling cascades that intersect with EGFR pathways. In cancer biology, Fluoxetine has been shown to **inhibit the ERK/MAPK pathway** and downstream proliferation signals ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18836303/#:~:text=manner,cancer%20cell%20proliferation%20during%20FLX)). ERK (extracellular signal–regulated kinase) is a core component of the EGFR signaling network. A 2009 study found that Fluoxetine at higher concentrations reduced phosphorylation of ERK1/2 in lung (A549) and colon (HT29) cells within 24 hours, leading to lower expression of pro-growth transcription factors (c-Myc, c-Fos) and cell cycle proteins ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18836303/#:~:text=manner,cancer%20cell%20proliferation%20during%20FLX)). By **suppressing ERK activation**, Fluoxetine effectively slowed cell growth in vitro ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18836303/#:~:text=phosphorylation%20of%20ERK1%2F2%20kinases%20in,during%20FLX%20treatment%20in%20vitro)). This is biologically relevant because **SARS-CoV-2 activation of EGFR** drives the same MAPK/ERK pathway to promote viral entry and replication ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37402592/#:~:text=signaling%20pathway%20%28MAPK%29%2C%20in%20SARS,2%20infection)). If Fluoxetine dampens ERK signaling, it could indirectly counteract an EGFR-mediated pro-viral mechanism. Indeed, researchers have proposed SSRIs might exert **antiviral effects via host-cell modulation**: Fluoxetine is a lysosomotropic agent that disrupts endosomal pH and cholesterol trafficking, potentially impeding viral genome release ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=accelerate%20drug%20repurposing%20efforts%2C%20which,were%20performed%20to%20assess%20the)). Additionally, Fluoxetine’s **sigma-1 receptor agonism** is linked to reducing cytokine production and may influence growth factor signaling crosstalk. While Fluoxetine is not known to directly reduce EGFR protein levels (as an EGFR inhibitor would), its broad effects (ERK inhibition, inflammation reduction) overlap with **downstream consequences of EGFR blockade**. In summary, it is **biologically plausible** that Fluoxetine attenuates some EGFR-driven processes – such as abnormal cell activation or inflammatory cascades – even though Fluoxetine doesn’t specifically target EGFR. This plausibility is supported by in vitro data and Fluoxetine’s recognized **anti-inflammatory mechanism**: for example, Fluoxetine can inhibit NF-κB and IL-6 signaling in immune cells ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33691249/#:~:text=Hyperinflammatory%20response%20caused%20by%20infections,knockdown%29%20cell%20lines)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33691249/#:~:text=in%20order%20to%20identify%20a,19)), and one study identified **IL6ST (gp130) and NFKB1** as critical mediators of Fluoxetine’s anti-inflammatory effect in a SARS-CoV-2 hyperinflammation model ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33691249/#:~:text=IL6%20signal%20transduction%20protein%20,19)). Since EGFR activation in lung cells can amplify NF-κB and IL-6 production, Fluoxetine’s effect here aligns with an EGFR-related pathway. 

Notably, in *vivo* and clinical evidence on Fluoxetine’s antiviral or EGFR-modulating activity is still limited. However, a related SSRI, **Fluvoxamine**, demonstrated clinical benefit in COVID-19 trials, presumably via similar immunomodulatory routes. In a 2020 randomized trial (Lenze *et al.*, *JAMA*, Nov 12, 2020), none of 80 patients on Fluvoxamine saw clinical deterioration versus 6 of 72 on placebo, suggesting SSRIs can prevent worsening of COVID-19 ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=The%20first%20clinical%20trial%20was,or%20dyspnoea)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=Results%20showed%20that%20no%20patient,appeared%20that%20fluvoxamine%20could%20be)). Though that trial didn’t measure EGFR or viral load directly, it implies SSRIs have physiologic effects in COVID beyond treating depression. Taken together, we have **indirect support** that Fluoxetine could influence EGFR’s role in COVID-19: by **inhibiting ERK/MAPK signaling** downstream of EGFR ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18836303/#:~:text=manner,cancer%20cell%20proliferation%20during%20FLX)) and by ameliorating the inflammatory milieu that EGFR activation contributes to. This step is **plausible but not fully proven** – the exact “Fluoxetine→EGFR” interaction has not been explicitly described in literature, yet the known pharmacology of Fluoxetine is compatible with dampening EGFR-related pathways (ERK, NF-κB, IL-6) during SARS-CoV-2 infection.

### 2. EGFR’s Interaction with Dexamethasone  
There is documented **crosstalk between EGFR signaling and glucocorticoid (dexamethasone) signaling** in cells. Dexamethasone, via the glucocorticoid receptor (GR), can modulate the expression and activity of growth factor receptors like EGFR. For instance, experiments in lung development found **Dexamethasone down-regulates EGFR gene expression** in fetal lung cells ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1748717/#:~:text=Retinoic%20acid%20and%20dexamethasone%20have,use%20of%20cycloheximide%20and%20actinomycin)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1748717/#:~:text=synthesis%20or%20125I,not%20retinoic%20acid%2C%20dexamethasone%2C%20or)). In that 1991 study, treating fetal rat lung cultures with Dexamethasone for 48 hours caused a ~60% decrease in EGFR mRNA levels, meaning the steroid **reduced EGFR synthesis** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1748717/#:~:text=Retinoic%20acid%20and%20dexamethasone%20have,use%20of%20cycloheximide%20and%20actinomycin)). This suggests Dexamethasone can make cells less responsive to EGF signaling by lowering receptor availability. Mechanistically, glucocorticoids often induce regulatory proteins (like MAPK phosphatases or IkB) that counter growth factor and NF-κB pathways, which may explain the reduced EGFR expression. Another study in cultured hepatocytes (Herrera *et al.*, 2003) showed Dexamethasone can have **biphasic effects on EGFR/ErbB family** – initially increasing some tyrosine kinase signaling but later contributing to feedback suppression of that pathway ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17585012/#:~:text=Dexamethasone%20modulates%20ErbB%20tyrosine%20kinase,studied%20the%20effects%20of%20dexamethasone)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6572202/#:~:text=Glucocorticoid%20Receptor%20Modulates%20EGFR%20Feedback,2)). Moreover, a 2023 report noted **ligand-activated GR inhibits EGF-induced cell migration** in epithelial cells ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4205848/#:~:text=Diurnal%20suppression%20of%20EGFR%20signalling,hormone%20signalling%2C%20we%20plated%20cells)), indicating functional antagonism between GR activation and EGFR signaling outputs. 

In the context of SARS-CoV-2, this interaction is intriguing: EGFR activation aids viral infection ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39291996/#:~:text=upregulation%20of%20the%20epidermal%20growth,cells%20infected%20with%20the%20SARS)), and Dexamethasone is now standard care to reduce severe COVID inflammation ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=Dexamethasone%2C%20a%20potent%20corticosteroid%2C%20gained,regarding%20potential%20side%20effects%20and)). The Engler 2023 study on EGFR-MAPK in COVID-19 even included experiments with A549 cells lacking the glucocorticoid receptor (GR knockout) to examine how absence of GR affected ACE2 and EGFR signaling ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37402592/#:~:text=signaling%20pathway%20%28MAPK%29%2C%20in%20SARS,2%20infection)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37402592/#:~:text=Graphical%20abstract%20Figure%20S1..%20Spike,MAPK%20signaling)). While detailed results of the GR knockout experiment were not given in the abstract, the inclusion implies researchers were testing whether glucocorticoid signaling (as would be activated by Dexamethasone) alters EGFR’s response to virus. It is known that **Dexamethasone usage in severe COVID-19 correlates with reduced inflammatory markers** and possibly viral load over time, and one mechanism could be via dampening pathways like EGFR that the virus hijacks ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37402592/#:~:text=signaling%20pathway%20%28MAPK%29%2C%20in%20SARS,2%20infection)). In summary, **EGFR and Dexamethasone pathways intersect at multiple points:** Dexamethasone tends to **suppress EGFR expression and downstream signals** in various tissues ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1748717/#:~:text=Retinoic%20acid%20and%20dexamethasone%20have,use%20of%20cycloheximide%20and%20actinomycin)), and conversely EGFR activity (or the need for EGFR in viral replication) could make the virus more susceptible to the effects of Dexamethasone. This reciprocal influence is biologically plausible and partially supported by experimental data. It’s reasonable to say that **“EGFR affects Dexamethasone”** (and vice-versa) in the sense that high EGFR-driven activity might exacerbate inflammation which Dexamethasone seeks to control, and Dexamethasone’s efficacy could stem from its ability to counteract growth factor-mediated tissue damage. The **exact step “EGFR → affects → Dexamethasone”** in the pathway likely refers to how EGFR pathway modulation changes the inflammatory environment that Dexamethasone targets. While this step hasn’t been described in those exact terms, the literature supports that **most steps in this sub-pathway are plausible:** EGFR activation contributes to COVID-19 pathology ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39291996/#:~:text=upregulation%20of%20the%20epidermal%20growth,cells%20infected%20with%20the%20SARS)), and Dexamethasone attenuates such pathology in part by suppressing growth factor-mediated inflammation ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1748717/#:~:text=Retinoic%20acid%20and%20dexamethasone%20have,use%20of%20cycloheximide%20and%20actinomycin)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4205848/#:~:text=Diurnal%20suppression%20of%20EGFR%20signalling,hormone%20signalling%2C%20we%20plated%20cells)).

### 3. Dexamethasone’s Contribution to Long COVID Outcomes  
Does Dexamethasone in acute COVID-19 influence the development of long COVID? This question has been examined in recent clinical studies with somewhat mixed results. **Biologically**, it stands to reason that reducing the severity of acute illness and inflammation (as Dexamethasone does) should reduce long-term tissue damage and sequelae. Persistent inflammation is one hypothesized driver of long COVID symptoms ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=thought%20to%20be%20key%20drivers,of%20these%20persistent%20symptoms)), and mitigating the initial “cytokine storm” might prevent a cascade of chronic immune dysregulation. Supporting this, a UK observational study (Milne *et al.*, preprint in 2021, later *Wellcome Open Res.*) compared patients hospitalized with COVID-19 before and after Dexamethasone became standard-of-care. At 8 months post-hospitalization, the **Dexamethasone-treated group had significantly fewer persistent symptoms** on average than those who did not receive steroids ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=Key%20Points)) ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=,2%20symptoms%20per%20patient)). Specifically, patients given Dexamethasone reported **about 1.9 ongoing symptoms vs. 3.2 symptoms in the non-steroid group** (p = 0.01) ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=after%20eight%20months%20than%20those,who%20did%20not%20receive%20it)). They also had notably lower prevalence of insomnia and a trend toward less fatigue long-term ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=significantly%20lower%20in%20the%20dexamethasone,2%20symptoms%20per%20patient)) ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=,significantly%20less%20common%20in%20the)). More Dexamethasone-treated patients were completely symptom-free at 8 months (41% vs 25%), although that difference didn’t reach statistical significance ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=,significantly%20less%20common%20in%20the)). These data suggest an **association between Dexamethasone use and reduced long COVID symptom burden**, at least in survivors of severe COVID. The authors concluded that proactively managing severe inflammation may improve long-term outcomes ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=%2A%20Acute%20Treatment%2C%20Long,term%20symptoms)) ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=%2A%20Acute%20Treatment%2C%20Long,term%20symptoms)).

However, other studies have not found a strong protective effect of steroids. A French prospective cohort study (Chan Sui Ko *et al.*, *Int. J. Gen. Med.*, Aug 18, 2022) followed 306 hospitalized COVID patients and reported **no significant difference** in 4-month persistent symptom rates between those who received corticosteroids acutely and those who did not ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9394663/#:~:text=a%20structured%20face,19%20in%20discharged%20patients)). In that study, ~63% of patients had ≥1 lingering symptom at 4 months, and steroid use (mostly Dexamethasone 6 mg for ≥6 days) did not reduce this proportion (OR = 1.00, p = 0.99) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9394663/#:~:text=a%20structured%20face,19%20in%20discharged%20patients)). Similarly, a large 2024 longitudinal cohort (*ERJ Open Res.*, Sep 2024) found **no clear long-term mortality benefit** at 1 year for ICU patients given Dexamethasone, though that focused on survival, not quality of life ([bmcinfectdis.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcinfectdis.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12879-024-10216-3#:~:text=The%20impact%20of%20dexamethasone%20on,year%29%20mortality.%20We%20found)). The discrepancy between studies could be due to differences in patient populations (the UK study specifically looked at those on oxygen in early 2020 vs. the French study included a broader group in 2020), follow-up duration, or sample size. It’s possible that **Dexamethasone’s effect on long COVID emerges more clearly over a longer time frame** (8–12 months) or in those with severe disease (where inflammation is highest). Indeed, a recent review notes that while steroids undoubtedly save lives in acute COVID ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=Dexamethasone%2C%20a%20potent%20corticosteroid%2C%20gained,regarding%20potential%20side%20effects%20and)), their ability to prevent long COVID is still uncertain and under investigation ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9394663/#:~:text=No%20Impact%20of%20Corticosteroid%20Use,patients%20who%20have%20been%20discharged)) ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=%2A%20Acute%20Treatment%2C%20Long,term%20symptoms)).

From a mechanistic viewpoint, Dexamethasone could **“contribute to” better long COVID outcomes** by preventing some of the root causes of PASC: e.g. minimizing lung fibrosis, autoimmunity, and endothelial damage. By suppressing EGFR and other pathways early (as discussed above), it might reduce the development of fibrosis or microthrombi that lead to chronic symptoms. There is also the consideration of viral persistence – overly high steroid doses might in theory impair viral clearance, but the standard 6 mg Dexamethasone regimen has not been linked to prolonged viremia in COVID-19 survivors. On balance, **this step of the pathway is backed by at least *some* literature support**: Dexamethasone’s acute benefits are well-described and one controlled observational study demonstrates improved long-term symptom profiles ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=,2%20symptoms%20per%20patient)). While not all data concur, the **biological plausibility is high** that effective immunosuppression in acute COVID (such as with Dexamethasone) *reduces* the risk or severity of long COVID. It’s important to clarify that “contribute to disease: long COVID-19” in the pathway likely means *influencing the disease course* rather than causing it – in this case, meaning Dexamethasone contributes to a reduction in long COVID incidence or severity. This interpretation aligns with clinical intentions: using Dexamethasone to *contribute to the prevention* of long-term complications ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=%2A%20Acute%20Treatment%2C%20Long,term%20symptoms)).

### 4. Overall Link: Fluoxetine and Long COVID  
Bringing the pieces together, we consider the ultimate question: **Can Fluoxetine realistically benefit patients with long COVID, given what we know about its interaction with EGFR-related pathways and parallels to Dexamethasone?** Recent research and expert opinions suggest that **yes, it is plausible**, and initial data even provide some supportive evidence. 

**Observational Evidence:** In 2023–2024, multiple large retrospective studies examined whether patients on SSRIs during acute COVID had different long COVID outcomes. Notably, Sidky *et al.* (published Jan 2024, *Comput. Struct. Biotech. J.*) used the U.S. N3C data of ~18,000 COVID patients to find that **baseline SSRI use was associated with a significantly lower risk of developing PASC** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10839808/#:~:text=Of%20the%2017%2C908%20patients%20identified%2C,of%20PASC%20was%20assessed)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10839808/#:~:text=A%2029,0.93%5D%3B%20P%C2%A0%3D%C2%A00.005)). They reported about a **29% reduction in long COVID risk** (RR ≈ 0.70) for SSRIs known to agonize sigma-1 receptors (like Fluoxetine and Fluvoxamine) and a **21% risk reduction** (RR ≈ 0.79) for SSRIs without sigma-1 activity (e.g. sertraline), compared to patients not on SSRIs ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10839808/#:~:text=Of%20the%2017%2C908%20patients%20identified%2C,of%20PASC%20was%20assessed)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10839808/#:~:text=A%2029,0.93%5D%3B%20P%C2%A0%3D%C2%A00.005)). Similarly, Fenton *et al.* found a statistically significant decrease in long COVID incidence among both groups of SSRI users, and no significant difference between SSRIs with versus without sigma-1 effects ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=P%E2%80%89%3D%E2%80%895%E2%80%89%C3%97%E2%80%8910,CI%200.62%E2%80%930.90)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=P%E2%80%89%3D%E2%80%895%E2%80%89%C3%97%E2%80%8910,CI%200.62%E2%80%930.90)). Another study focusing on patients with depression (BMC Medicine, Oct 2024) found **SSRI users had a modest but significant 8% lower relative risk of long COVID** over 12 months (adjusted RR ~0.92) compared to non-users ([bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x#:~:text=We%20analyzed%20a%20sample%20,CI%20%280.80%2C%200.98)). Interestingly, new SSRI users (who started the antidepressant just before infection) saw about a **11% lower risk** of long COVID (RR ~0.89) ([bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x#:~:text=We%20analyzed%20a%20sample%20,CI%20%280.80%2C%200.98)). These real-world findings bolster the plausibility: Fluoxetine, as a commonly used SSRI, appears to have *protective associations* against long COVID in large patient cohorts.

**Mechanistic Rationale:** From a mechanistic standpoint, Fluoxetine might replicate some of Dexamethasone’s beneficial actions via a different route. While Dexamethasone broadly shuts down immune gene transcription, Fluoxetine **modulates immune function more subtly**. It can polarize macrophages towards an anti-inflammatory state ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=Some%20of%20the%20potentially%20relevant,42)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=inflammatory%20response%20induced%20by%20a,42)), reduce platelet aggregation (thus possibly preventing microclots implicated in long COVID) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=In%20severe%20COVID,fluoxetine%20appears%20to%20prevent%20platelet)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=fluoxetine%20and%20fluvoxamine%20block%20the,47)), and maintain higher serotonin levels which might counteract the serotonin depletion observed in long COVID patients ([bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x#:~:text=Long%20COVID%2C%20also%20known%20as,depression%20can%20reduce%20the%20concern)). The **serotonin connection** is especially noteworthy: A recent *Cell* study (Aug 2023) found that persistent viral antigen in the gut triggers interferon that lowers blood serotonin, contributing to multisystem symptoms of long COVID ([bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x#:~:text=Long%20COVID%2C%20also%20known%20as,depression%20can%20reduce%20the%20concern)). SSRIs like Fluoxetine **increase synaptic and circulating serotonin**, which could directly alleviate some of these downstream effects (e.g. improving vagal tone, memory, and vasodilation) ([bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x#:~:text=Long%20COVID%2C%20also%20known%20as,depression%20can%20reduce%20the%20concern)) ([bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x#:~:text=often%20have%20debilitating%20consequences,concern%20of%20confounding%20by%20indication)). This hypothesis was significant enough that the BMC Medicine authors specifically restricted their sample to depressed patients (where SSRIs are indicated) to reduce bias, and concluded the data *“support the hypothesis that serotonin may be a key mechanistic biomarker of Long COVID”* and that SSRIs could be protective ([bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x#:~:text=Conclusions)). 

**Expert Opinions:** Leading researchers have begun to advocate for testing SSRIs as a therapy for long COVID. Kenji Hashimoto (a neuropsychopharmacology expert) wrote in March 2023 that **fluvoxamine (and by extension SSRIs)** could be “a potential therapeutic or prophylactic drug for … long COVID” due to its combined **serotonergic, sigma-1, and anti-inflammatory actions** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=potential%20therapeutic%20drug%20for%20long,of%20interest%20to%20investigate%20whether)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=be%20a%20potential%20therapeutic%20or,the%20author%20would%20like%20to)). In his review, Hashimoto noted that both sigma-1 agonist SSRIs like fluvoxamine/fluoxetine and non-agonists showed reduced long COVID risks in analyses, indicating a class effect ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=P%E2%80%89%3D%E2%80%895%E2%80%89%C3%97%E2%80%8910,CI%200.62%E2%80%930.90)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=significant%20reduction%20in%20relative%20risk,inflammatory)). He points out SSRIs’ **favorable safety profile, low cost, and global availability** as strong advantages for repurposing in long COVID ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=be%20a%20potential%20therapeutic%20or,the%20author%20would%20like%20to)). Other experts writing in *Lancet Infectious Diseases* and *PNAS* have highlighted SSRIs among the promising leads for long COVID treatment, though calling for randomized trials to prove efficacy ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=after%20infection%20and%20a%20reduction,10%2C%2011%2C%20106%2C%20107)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=taste,19%20survivors)). It’s worth noting that at this time, **no official guidelines recommend SSRIs specifically to treat long COVID** – any use for this purpose is off-label and based on mechanism or limited evidence. But the converging insights from immunology, virology, and clinical data make it *reasonable* to suspect Fluoxetine could help: It tackles some of the same biological problems (e.g. excess IL-6, hypercoagulation, low serotonin) that are believed to underpin long COVID ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=In%20severe%20COVID,fluoxetine%20appears%20to%20prevent%20platelet)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=increased%20activation%2C%20reactivity%2C%20and%20aggregation,47)). 

**Real-World Implementation:** Already, some long COVID patients with co-morbid depression or anxiety are prescribed Fluoxetine or other SSRIs as part of standard psychiatric care. This offers an opportunity to observe any ancillary benefits on long COVID symptoms. Additionally, SSRIs were used in acute COVID trials (e.g., fluvoxamine in the TOGETHER trial, 2021) and showed reduced hospitalization rates ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=The%20first%20clinical%20trial%20was,or%20dyspnoea)). Though these trials targeted acute outcomes, a follow-up of those patients could reveal if the SSRI arm has fewer long-term complaints. Meanwhile, Dexamethasone remains a **standard acute treatment**, and millions worldwide have received it during COVID-19 surges ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=Dexamethasone%2C%20a%20potent%20corticosteroid%2C%20gained,regarding%20potential%20side%20effects%20and)). By reducing deaths and severe organ damage, dexamethasone likely indirectly reduced some burden of long COVID (since those who might have had the worst sequelae survived with less damage). If Fluoxetine can mimic even part of dexamethasone’s effect (for example, by blunting the inflammatory response early in infection as some propose ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=Some%20of%20the%20potentially%20relevant,42))), it would strengthen the case for plausibility. To date, **no dedicated clinical trial has tested Fluoxetine for preventing or treating long COVID**, but multiple teams have called for such studies ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=potential%20therapeutic%20drug%20for%20long,of%20interest%20to%20investigate%20whether)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=after%20infection%20and%20a%20reduction,10%2C%2011%2C%20106%2C%20107)).

## Relevant Data and Statistics  

To summarize key data supporting this Fluoxetine–long COVID connection:

- **Long COVID prevalence:** ~10–20% of COVID-19 patients experience lingering symptoms for months ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10839808/#:~:text=Post,5%5D%20These%20symptoms%20may)). This includes a substantial fraction of even mild outpatient cases, though risk is higher after severe illness. For example, one study found ~33% of COVID survivors had at least one symptom at 3–6 months post-infection ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10839808/#:~:text=patients%20with%20COVID,6)).

- **Fluoxetine/SSRI use and long COVID risk:** In a January 2024 N3C database study (17,908 patients, USA), baseline SSRI use was linked to a **21–29% lower relative risk of long COVID** (PASC) over 6+ months ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10839808/#:~:text=A%2029,0.93%5D%3B%20P%C2%A0%3D%C2%A00.005)). The reduction was significant for both Fluoxetine/Fluvoxamine (sigma-1 agonists) and other SSRIs ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10839808/#:~:text=A%2029,0.93%5D%3B%20P%C2%A0%3D%C2%A00.005)). A separate October 2024 study (302,626 patients with depression) found SSRI users had an adjusted long COVID risk of 0.92 (95% CI 0.86–0.99) compared to non-users ([bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x#:~:text=We%20analyzed%20a%20sample%20,CI%20%280.80%2C%200.98)).

- **Mechanistic findings:** Fluoxetine demonstrates multiple biological activities that align with combating long COVID’s proposed mechanisms: it **inhibits IL-6 and NF-κB** (key drivers of cytokine storm) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33691249/#:~:text=Hyperinflammatory%20response%20caused%20by%20infections,we%20compare%20transcriptomic%20signatures%20from)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33691249/#:~:text=in%20order%20to%20identify%20a,19)); it **accumulates in lungs** and can interfere with viral replication in vitro ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=accelerate%20drug%20repurposing%20efforts%2C%20which,were%20performed%20to%20assess%20the)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=severe%20COVID,body%20fluid%20concentrations%20with%20the)); it **prevents platelet aggregation** which might reduce microclots ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8998734/#:~:text=In%20severe%20COVID,fluoxetine%20appears%20to%20prevent%20platelet)); and it **boosts serotonin levels, countering the low-serotonin state** observed in long COVID patients ([bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x#:~:text=Long%20COVID%2C%20also%20known%20as,depression%20can%20reduce%20the%20concern)).

- **Dexamethasone outcomes:** The RECOVERY trial (July 2020) showed Dexamethasone cut mortality by ~33% in ventilated patients and ~20% in those on oxygen ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=Dexamethasone%2C%20a%20potent%20corticosteroid%2C%20gained,regarding%20potential%20side%20effects%20and)). Regarding long-term symptoms, an 8-month follow-up observational study reported **significantly fewer long COVID symptoms** in patients who received Dexamethasone during acute illness (mean 1.9 vs 3.2 symptoms, p=0.01) ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=,2%20symptoms%20per%20patient)). However, a 4-month follow-up study in 2020–21 found **no significant difference** in persistent symptom rates with steroid use (63% vs 63%, p=0.99) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9394663/#:~:text=a%20structured%20face,19%20in%20discharged%20patients)), underscoring the need for more research on timing, dosage, and patient selection for steroid benefits.

- **EGFR and SARS-CoV-2:** Lab studies (Engler 2023; Kastenbaum 2023) indicate SARS-CoV-2 exploits EGFR: **Spike protein activates EGFR/MAPK**, and using an EGFR inhibitor (osimertinib) reduced infection of cells by wild-type and Omicron variant virus ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39291996/#:~:text=upregulation%20of%20the%20epidermal%20growth,cells%20infected%20with%20the%20SARS)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39291996/#:~:text=pseudoviral%20particles%20to%20guide%20future,Furthermore%2C%20this%20inhibitory%20effect)). This points to ~new therapeutic avenues (e.g. repurposing cancer EGFR inhibitors for COVID) and validates EGFR as a biologically relevant target in COVID pathology ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39291996/#:~:text=novel%20variants%20that%20have%20heightened,a%20reduction%20in%20cell%20viability)).

- **Expert consensus:** Reviews and perspective articles in 2023 (e.g. Hashimoto 2023 in *Discover Mental Health*) highlight SSRIs as one of the “few promising” pharmacological approaches for long COVID, given their immunomodulatory effects and safety profile ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=after%20infection%20and%20a%20reduction,10%2C%2011%2C%20106%2C%20107)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=be%20a%20potential%20therapeutic%20or,the%20author%20would%20like%20to)). However, experts caution that **controlled trials** are required to confirm efficacy in long COVID ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10029802/#:~:text=taste,19%20survivors)). 

## Conclusion and Plausibility Score  

Considering all the above, we assess the **biological plausibility** of the Fluoxetine–long COVID relationship via the proposed EGFR/Dexamethasone pathway to be **strongly positive**. In terms of the scoring criteria:

- **Exact mechanism described?** – The precise full pathway (Fluoxetine → EGFR → Dexamethasone → long COVID) has **not been explicitly documented as a single mechanism in literature**. There is no paper yet that outlines “Fluoxetine treats long COVID by affecting EGFR and mimicking dexamethasone.” Thus, the *exact multi-step chain is hypothetical*. However, **each component of the chain is supported by scientific findings:** Fluoxetine’s anti-inflammatory and cell signaling effects are well-documented ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33691249/#:~:text=IL6%20signal%20transduction%20protein%20,19)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18836303/#:~:text=manner,cancer%20cell%20proliferation%20during%20FLX)), EGFR’s role in COVID-19 is described ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37402592/#:~:text=signaling%20pathway%20%28MAPK%29%2C%20in%20SARS,2%20infection)), Dexamethasone’s acute benefits are proven ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=Dexamethasone%2C%20a%20potent%20corticosteroid%2C%20gained,regarding%20potential%20side%20effects%20and)), and links to long COVID are suggested by multiple studies ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=,2%20symptoms%20per%20patient)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10839808/#:~:text=A%2029,0.93%5D%3B%20P%C2%A0%3D%C2%A00.005)).

- **Support for each step:** **Most steps have at least some support**. There is evidence that Fluoxetine can dampen pathways that overlap with EGFR signaling (ERK/NF-κB) and evidence that EGFR activation worsens COVID while EGFR inhibition improves outcomes ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39291996/#:~:text=upregulation%20of%20the%20epidermal%20growth,cells%20infected%20with%20the%20SARS)) ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39291996/#:~:text=pseudoviral%20particles%20to%20guide%20future,Furthermore%2C%20this%20inhibitory%20effect)). The interplay of EGFR and GR (dexamethasone) is biologically plausible with supporting data on gene expression and cellular behavior ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1748717/#:~:text=Retinoic%20acid%20and%20dexamethasone%20have,use%20of%20cycloheximide%20and%20actinomycin)) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4205848/#:~:text=Diurnal%20suppression%20of%20EGFR%20signalling,hormone%20signalling%2C%20we%20plated%20cells)). Dexamethasone’s effect on long COVID is somewhat supported (one study positive ([medx.it.com](https://medx.it.com/understanding-the-impact-of-dexamethasone-on-persistent-symptoms-of-covid-19-an-observational-study#:~:text=,2%20symptoms%20per%20patient)), one negative ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9394663/#:~:text=a%20structured%20face,19%20in%20discharged%20patients)), but theory supports it). Finally, Fluoxetine/SSRIs and long COVID have **emerging supportive evidence** in observational human studies ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10839808/#:~:text=A%2029,0.93%5D%3B%20P%C2%A0%3D%C2%A00.005)) ([bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-024-03655-x#:~:text=We%20analyzed%20a%20sample%20,CI%20%280.80%2C%200.98)).
